About Newark-on-Trent

One of England's most historically significant market towns, sitting at the crossroads of ancient highways on the banks of the River Trent.

A Journey Through Time

Newark's story stretches back to Roman times, through medieval grandeur, civil war and beyond.

Roman Era

Ad Pontem

The Romans established a settlement called Ad Pontem ("at the bridge") near modern Newark, recognising the strategic importance of the River Trent crossing. The Fosse Way, one of the great Roman roads connecting Exeter to Lincoln, passed directly through the area.

Anglo-Saxon Period

The Birth of Newark

The name "Newark" likely derives from "New Work," referring to a new fortification built during the Anglo-Saxon era. The settlement grew as a market town and a key crossing point on the River Trent, establishing the foundations of its commercial importance.

c.1133

The Castle Rises

Alexander, Bishop of Lincoln, founded Newark Castle as an episcopal fortress. Initially an earth-and-timber motte-and-bailey structure, it was rebuilt in stone towards the end of the 12th century in red sandstone, with a magnificent gatehouse that remains one of the finest examples of Romanesque architecture in England.

1216

Death of King John

King John, weakened by dysentery and reportedly devastated after losing the crown jewels in the Wash, arrived at Newark Castle in October 1216. He died here on the night of 18th October, making Newark forever associated with one of England's most infamous monarchs. The castle's role as a royal residence cemented its national importance.

1549

Market Charter

Newark received its market charter, formalising centuries of trading activity in the great Market Square. The market tradition continues to this day, with the square remaining one of the finest and largest in England.

1643–1646

English Civil War

Newark was a Royalist stronghold and its strategic position controlling the crossing of the Trent and the Great North Road made it a prize worth fighting for. The town endured three sieges between 1643 and 1646, finally surrendering only on the direct orders of King Charles I. The Queen's Sconce, an impressive star-shaped earthwork fortification built during the war, survives as a remarkable monument in the south of the town.

18th–19th Century

Georgian Growth

Newark flourished as a coaching town on the Great North Road between London and Edinburgh. The elegant Town Hall was built in 1776, and the arrival of the railway in 1846 brought further prosperity. The town's brewing industry thrived, and fine Georgian and Victorian buildings sprang up around the Market Square.

Today

Modern Newark

Newark is a vibrant market town of around 42,000 people in the Newark and Sherwood district. Served by two railway stations — including Newark North Gate on the East Coast Main Line — the town enjoys excellent connections while retaining its distinctive character. The opening of the National Civil War Centre in 2015 and ongoing heritage investment reflect a town that's proud of its past and confident about its future.

Newark Castle

Standing proudly on the banks of the River Trent, Newark Castle is the town's most iconic landmark. Founded around 1133 by Alexander, Bishop of Lincoln, the castle was rebuilt in red sandstone and developed into one of the finest fortified residences in medieval England.

The gatehouse, with its elegant Romanesque arches, is considered one of the best-preserved 12th-century gatehouses in the country. Visitors can explore the castle grounds and gardens for free, take guided tours of the towers and underground passages, and enjoy stunning views over the river.

The castle is a Grade I listed building and scheduled monument, recognised as a structure of international importance.

Plan Your Visit

The Church of St Mary Magdalene

Dominating the skyline with its magnificent 252-foot (77m) spire, the Church of St Mary Magdalene is built on the scale of a cathedral. Dating from the 12th century, this Grade I listed church is one of the grandest parish churches in England.

Among its treasures is a large Flemish brass commemorating Alan Fleming, a wealthy wool merchant who died in 1375. Measuring over 9 feet tall, it is one of the largest of its kind in the country. The church is a testament to the wealth that Newark's medieval wool and cloth trade brought to the town.

Newark Today

A thriving market town that honours its heritage while looking to the future.

Population

Around 42,000 people call the Newark and Sherwood district home, with a close-knit community feel that's often remarked upon by newcomers.

Location

Situated in Nottinghamshire at the junction of the A1 and A46, on the East Coast Main Line. London King's Cross is just 1 hour 20 minutes by train.

Character

A genuine market town with independent shops, excellent pubs, a thriving arts scene, and a Market Square that has been the heart of the community for centuries.

Notable People

Newark has been home to — or closely associated with — a number of notable figures throughout history.

William Ewart Gladstone

The four-time Prime Minister was MP for Newark from 1832 to 1845 and made his first public speech at the Clinton Arms on the Market Square. His early political career was shaped in the town.

King John

While not from Newark, King John's death at the castle in 1216 is one of the most significant events in the town's history and in English medieval history.

Sir Donald Wolfit

The acclaimed Shakespearean actor was born in Newark in 1902. He was considered one of the last great actor-managers and was knighted for his services to the theatre.

Magnus the Magnificent

Thomas Magnus, a Tudor diplomat and priest, founded the Magnus Grammar School in Newark in 1530. The school — now The Magnus Church of England Academy — continues his educational legacy today.
